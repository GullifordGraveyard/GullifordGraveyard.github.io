Friends of Gulliford Burial Ground


About the group:

The Gulliford Burial Ground is a dissenters' burial ground managed by both Lympstone and Woodbury Parish councils as it is situated on the boundary of both parishes and is important to the history of both.

The Friends of Gulliford Burial Ground has been set up by local residents who love the site as a beautiful and tranquil place to visit and also to preserve the history and heritage of the site. It has created an avenue for the Parish councils to get the volunteer helpers it needs to keep the site in good repair though gardening working parties and fundraising events.

Adverse weather conditions and lack of funds has lead to the burial ground's walls needing urgent repair. The graves also need to be repaired and maintained and we hope that we can now move forward to fundraise in order to do these urgent repairs and preserve this historical site for the next generation of parishioners.

The History of Gulliford Burial Ground

Gulliford Burial Ground lies in Meetings Lane (reference to the Meeting House) and the top of Strawberry Hill, Lympstone.

A Presbyterian and later Unitarian site going back to the 17th century to a time of religious upheaval and risk of imprisonment to those not toeing the religious line. The first chapel was built in 1687, the band of dissenters grow made up by the new middle classes to humbler locals from Lympstone, Woodbury, Ebford, Exmouth & the surrounding areas, often with a dozen carriages parked outside, not an easy journey on unmade roads.

A violent riot broke out at one time, entering the chapel and smashing windows reminding the congregation of the risk of worshipping outside the established church. In 1774 a further plot of land was donated to extend the site for a second larger chapel to be built to replace the original chapel, which was now too small for the extra congregation. A ruling was make at the time that the new meeting house must not be used as a burying place for the dead with all burials performed in the graveyard (thence the lack of headstones at the back of the site today). Yearly donations from the congregation funded the site, Thomas Smith (Queen Anne House, Lympstone) gave Â£2 & Joseph Nutt of Lympstone gave 4 shillings in 1733.

In 1853, the cost of a marriage for a subscriber to the chapel was, 10s 6d for the Minister, 2s each for the Clerk & Sexton. Baptism was 1s for the Minister, 9d for both the Clerk & Seaton. By the end of the 19th century, the laws of dissenters had relaxed, new chapels were built in Exmouth & Topsham reducing the need to travel so far. The Methodists too had a new chapel, having for years hired the dis-used Dissenters chapel in Church Road, next to the Redwing (thence the former name The Vaults) and where Ellenthorpe now stands. The congregation was down to about ten with a minister who cancelled without notice, the roof was unsafe, repairs neglected and finally in 1907 the chapel was demolished.

Today the burial ground is jointly looked after by Lympstone & Woodbury Parish Councils. The perimeter walls and the Jervis, the Lees and the Stogdon tombs are Grade II Listed. Events 2018

Free Tours of Gulliford Burial Ground

The Friends of Gulliford Burial Ground are holding their first public event on the 11th & 12th of August (Saturday & Sunday) at 10.30am & 2.30pm each day.

The free tours will include an introduction to the history of the site and chapels by Gill Selley, followed by researched stories of some of the dissenters buried there from an artist to a whale merchant, by Angela Coles.
